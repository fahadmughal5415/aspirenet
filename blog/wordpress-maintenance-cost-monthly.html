How Much Does It Cost to Maintain a WordPress Website Every Month?

AspireNet Team June 2026 8 min read

Once a WordPress website is live, a new question usually appears within the first few months: what does it cost to actually keep it running? The build cost gets most of the attention, but the ongoing maintenance cost is what determines whether a website stays fast, secure, and useful for years, or slowly turns into a liability.

This guide breaks down exactly what WordPress maintenance involves, what it should cost on a monthly basis, and what happens if you skip it entirely.

What WordPress Maintenance Actually Covers

WordPress maintenance is not a single task. It is a combination of several recurring activities that keep a website functioning correctly. The most important of these is keeping WordPress core, themes, and plugins updated. Each update can include security patches, bug fixes, and compatibility improvements. Skipping updates for even a few months creates real vulnerabilities.

Beyond updates, maintenance includes regular backups so the site can be restored if something goes wrong, uptime monitoring to catch outages quickly, security scanning to detect malware or suspicious activity, and performance checks to make sure the site continues to load quickly as content and traffic grow.

Hosting is usually a separate cost from maintenance, typically running $10 to $50 per month depending on traffic and performance requirements. The maintenance fee is specifically for the ongoing work of keeping the site updated, secure, and backed up.

A common mistake is assuming that once a website is built, it requires no further investment. WordPress is open source software that receives updates constantly. A site that is never maintained becomes outdated and vulnerable within months, not years.

What Happens If You Skip Maintenance

The risks of skipping WordPress maintenance are not theoretical. Outdated plugins are one of the most common ways websites get hacked. A compromised website can be used to send spam, host malicious content, or redirect visitors to other sites, all without the owner immediately noticing.

Beyond security, outdated WordPress sites tend to slow down over time as plugins become incompatible with newer PHP versions, and eventually some plugins simply stop working altogether. A site that has not been updated in two or three years often needs a more expensive emergency fix than it would have cost to maintain properly the whole time.

Doing It Yourself vs Paying for Managed Maintenance

For a simple website with no eCommerce functionality, basic self-maintenance is possible. This means logging in monthly to update plugins and themes, checking that backups are running, and occasionally reviewing the site for issues. This requires roughly one to two hours per month and some comfort with WordPress.

For business websites, especially those generating leads or sales, a managed maintenance plan removes the risk of something breaking unnoticed. If an update causes a conflict and the site goes down, a managed plan typically catches and fixes this within hours rather than the owner discovering it days later when a customer mentions the site is broken.

How to Choose the Right Maintenance Plan

The right level of maintenance depends on how important the website is to the business. A simple portfolio site that generates no direct revenue can tolerate basic maintenance. A website that is the primary source of leads or sales for a business should have a proper maintenance plan, because even a few hours of downtime or a security breach can directly cost the business money.

When evaluating a maintenance plan, confirm exactly what is included. Some providers only handle updates, while others include backups, security monitoring, and a guaranteed response time if something breaks. The cheapest plan is not always the best value if it leaves gaps in coverage that matter for your specific website.
